## Core Takeaway

FCV is a highly variable RNA virus whose mechanism branch holds genome plasticity, receptor/tissue-tropism logic, persistence biology, and pathotype diversification in the same frame. This architecture explains why FCV control remains difficult despite widespread vaccination.

## Mechanism Hierarchy

The FCV mechanism spine follows this order:

### Layer 1: Diversity and Evolution

FCV demonstrates remarkable genetic, antigenic, and clinical diversity. Outbreak vaccine-resistant strains occur frequently. Sequence variation is part of the control problem, not just a taxonomy detail.

**Lead sources:** `src-fcv-001`, `src-fcv-002`, `src-fcv-015`, `src-fcv-023`, `src-fcv-032`

**European Epidemiology (src-fcv-032):**
- Multi-national European cross-sectional study of FCV strain diversity
- Vaccine cross-reactivity evaluated against circulating field strains
- Geographic variation affects expected vaccine efficacy

**Current safe read:**
- FCV is a highly variable positive-stranded RNA virus
- continuing genetic and antigenic diversification maintains vaccine pressure
- sequence diversity explains much of the control difficulty
- European strain diversity affects vaccine cross-protection expectations

### Layer 2: Receptor Entry and Tissue Distribution

Receptor biology (feline JAM-A/JAM-1) and tissue distribution help explain why FCV is broader than a simple upper-airway virus. Multiple clinical biotypes fit under the same molecular frame.

**Lead sources:** `src-fcv-015`, `src-fcv-016`, `src-fcv-034`

**Key finding (2006, src-fcv-034):** JAM-1 (junctional adhesion molecule 1) identified as the functional cellular receptor for FCV entry. This foundational discovery explains:
- Why FCV can infect multiple tissue types (JAM-1 is broadly expressed)
- Why FCV extends beyond upper respiratory sites
- Potential therapeutic targets (receptor-blocking strategies)

**Current safe read:**
- JAM-A/JAM-1 receptor distribution across feline tissues explains broad lesion diversity
- FCV should not be modeled as only an upper-respiratory virus
- receptor/tropism logic now sits under the mechanism spine rather than only under extension papers
- receptor identification enables entry-inhibitor antiviral development

### Layer 3: Persistence and Chronic Carriage

Persistence and chronic carriage are built into the FCV system model. Long-term transmission pressure is partly maintained by carrier-state biology.

**Lead sources:** `src-fcv-002`, `src-fcv-007`, `src-fcv-017`

**Current safe read:**
- persistence is a core FCV systems problem
- chronic carriers maintain population-level transmission
- acute-disease mitigation does not equal chronic-carrier control
- vaccination reduces disease burden more defensibly than it prevents carrier-state formation

### Layer 4: Pathotype Diversification (VS-FCV)

FCV generates multiple clinical forms, including virulent systemic disease (VS-FCV). Control strategy must account for heterogeneity in both virus and phenotype.

**Lead sources:** `src-fcv-001`, `src-fcv-007`, `src-fcv-015`, `src-fcv-025`, `src-fcv-026`, `src-fcv-027`, `src-fcv-045`

**VS-FCV Outbreak Evidence:**
- Multiple lethal outbreaks documented across US, Europe, Australia (src-fcv-025, src-fcv-026, src-fcv-027)
- Australian outbreak study included viral characterization + in vitro antiviral testing (src-fcv-027)
- High mortality in healthy adult cats: up to 67% (src-fcv-045)

**Mechanistic Finding (src-fcv-045):**
- VS-FCV pathogenesis involves cytokine dysregulation, not just viral invasion
- Affected skin tissues show on average 3.8 elevated cytokines vs controls
- Prominent upregulation: IL-10, TNF-α, MIP-1α
- Capsid gene mutations implicated in emerging virulent phenotype
- Suggests cytokine storm-like mechanism underlies VS-FCV lethality

**Current safe read:**
- classical FCV and VS-FCV are distinct pathotypes requiring different framing
- pathotype diversity partly flows from genome plasticity (capsid mutations drive virulence)
- severe-systemic disease exists but should not replace routine URTD as the default frame
- VS-FCV involves both viral evolution and host immune dysregulation

### Layer 5: Emerging Therapeutics

Beyond vaccines, FCV control may benefit from antiviral drugs and novel immunization platforms.

**Lead sources:** `src-fcv-028`, `src-fcv-038`, `src-fcv-027`

**mRNA Vaccine (src-fcv-028, 2025):**
- VP1 capsid protein-based mRNA vaccine developed
- Provides protection in FCV challenge study
- Novel platform beyond traditional inactivated/modified-live vaccines
- Potential for improved cross-protection (not yet confirmed in field)

**Entry Inhibitors (src-fcv-038):**
- Fexaramine identified as FCV entry blocker in vitro
- Mechanism: blocks viral binding to JAM-1 receptor
- Represents alternative therapeutic approach to vaccination

**VS-FCV Antivirals (src-fcv-027):**
- Australian outbreak study tested novel antivirals in vitro against VS-FCV isolates
- Demonstrates therapeutic development potential for severe disease

**Current safe read:**
- mRNA vaccine technology is applicable to FCV
- entry inhibitors represent mechanistically distinct therapeutic approach
- antivirals are at in vitro stage; clinical recommendations not yet supported

### Extension Branches (Oral, Ocular, Enteric)

Ocular, enteric, and chronic oral disease extend the FCV disease map without replacing the core respiratory presentation.

**Lead sources:** `src-fcv-020`, `src-fcv-021`, `src-fcv-029`, `src-fcv-039`

**Chronic Oral Disease (FCGS):**
- FCV associated with chronic feline gingivostomatitis (src-fcv-029, src-fcv-039)
- Multi-pathogen context: FHV-1, Bartonella, FeLV, FIV also associated
- FCV role in FORL (odontoclastic resorptive lesions) also studied
- Association established; causation complex

**Current safe read:**
- ocular-surface FCV is real and syndrome-aware
- enteric FCV association is visible but should not become a lead-cause shortcut
- chronic oral disease (FCGS) is a significant FCV-associated syndrome
- extension branches widen recognition without becoming first-line diagnostics

## Guardrail

Do not let one mechanistic slice dominate the page. FCV is not adequately explained by sequence diversity alone, receptor biology alone, or vaccine escape alone. The four-layer hierarchy (diversity → receptor/tropism → persistence → pathotype) is stronger than any single causal story.

## What The Module Can Say Safely

- FCV is best modeled as a variable, persistence-capable, multi-phenotype viral system
- control difficulty is mechanistically connected to diversity and persistence
- receptor/tissue-tropism logic matters for understanding lesion diversity
- **JAM-1 is the functional cellular receptor for FCV** (established finding)
- **VS-FCV involves cytokine dysregulation (IL-10, TNF-α, MIP-1α) with mortality up to 67%**
- **Capsid gene mutations are implicated in VS-FCV virulent phenotype**
- **FCV is associated with chronic gingivostomatitis (FCGS)** in multifactorial context
- mechanism writing should explain control problems, not float separately from them
- mRNA vaccines and entry inhibitors are in development (in vitro/challenge stage)

## What The Module Should Not Say Yet

- do not reduce FCV to a simple respiratory-virus description
- do not imply receptor distribution alone explains virulence
- do not treat persistence as synonymous with vaccine failure
- do not pretend the current source pack has a full strain-by-strain virulence map
- **do not recommend antivirals or mRNA vaccines clinically** (not yet validated in field)
- **do not claim FCGS is caused solely by FCV** (multifactorial etiology)
