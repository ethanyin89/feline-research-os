# Epidemiological features of feline mammary carcinoma

## Evidence-Depth Caveat

This card is based on complete abstract text and metadata. It is deep-extracted as an epidemiological and retrospective study.

## One-Line Summary

An epidemiological study of 132 cats with mammary neoplasia showing a 9:1 malignancy ratio, a 2-fold increased risk (P < 0.01) with earlier onset in Siamese cats, and a lack of estrogen dependency.

## Why It Matters For Feline Cancer

This foundational study establishes the high malignancy rate (90%) of feline mammary tumors, which differs dramatically from dogs (where ~50% are benign). It also identifies a strong genetic risk factor in Siamese cats and describes the lack of estrogen dependency in FMC, qualifying it as a model for triple-negative or hormone-receptor-negative human breast cancers.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

* "The ratio of malignant to benign tumours was 9:1."
* "There were 113 cases of carcinomas of all types (including two males), with adenocarcinoma being the predominant cell-type."
* "Relative risk analysis indicated that the Siamese breed had twice the risk (P less than 0.01) of developing mammary carcinoma compared to all breeds combined."
* "The age at diagnosis in Siamese females tended to be younger than in other breeds."

### source_supported_conclusion

* Feline mammary neoplasia is overwhelmingly malignant, with 90% of cases diagnosed as malignant carcinomas.
* Siamese cats have twice the relative risk of developing mammary carcinomas compared to the general feline population, with diagnosis occurring at a younger age.
* Feline mammary breast cancer generally lacks estrogen dependency.

### llm_inference

* Since feline mammary tumors are almost exclusively malignant and aggressive, any palpable mass in the feline mammary gland should be treated with high clinical suspicion and undergo cytological/biopsy verification.
