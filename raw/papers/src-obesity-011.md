# The cat as a model for human obesity: insights into depot-specific inflammation associated with feline obesity

## Evidence-Depth Caveat

This card has been upgraded from abstract-weighted intake to a user-supplied full deep extraction. It can support research-content claims about feline obesity, adipose inflammation, and model boundaries, but not broad clinical or treatment claims.

The linked raw extraction is the current passage owner:

- `raw/deep-extractions/ext-src-obesity-011.md`

## One-Line Summary

A small study of naturally obese cats found local adipose tissue inflammatory and adipokine changes, with a pattern that appears more subcutaneous-fat weighted than a simple human VAT-centric obesity model.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- The study compared 10 lean cats with 10 chronically obese cats.
- Obesity was naturally occurring rather than experimentally induced.
- Obese cats showed larger adipocytes and more adipose T lymphocytes, but not significant B lymphocyte or macrophage increases.
- CCL-5 and MCP-1 were increased in adipose tissue, while blood measures did not show the same group separation in the deep extraction.

### source_supported_conclusion

- This source supports a local-inflammation layer for feline obesity.
- It supports a depot-specific model-boundary claim: feline obesity is not a simple copy of human VAT-dominant inflammatory risk.
- It supports distinguishing tissue-level signals from circulating-marker claims.

### llm_inference

- The paper is useful for explaining why feline obesity should be treated as an endocrine/immune tissue state, not only as excess body weight.
- Its comparative-model value depends on preserving the differences between cat and human depot patterns.
- It should be paired with insulin-sensitivity and metabolic-phenotyping sources before making diabetes-risk architecture claims.

## Claim-Fit Judgment

Strong use:

- local tissue signal versus blood signal distinction
