# One-line Summary

Neurologic FIP treatment paper that should keep the modern antiviral branch from being modeled only around easier disease forms.

## Why It Matters For FIP

- prevents the treatment branch from collapsing into one undifferentiated efficacy story
- likely introduces distinct outcome, dosing, and practical-complexity issues
- now serves as the first deep-extracted neurologic-treatment anchor in the FIP module

## Key Findings

- abstract explicitly frames neurologic FIP as a branch with limited prior treatment data
- four CNS-involved cases were treated and monitored serially with physical, neurologic, and ophthalmic examinations
- one case included serial MRI, CSF analysis, PCR, and ocular imaging follow-up
- abstract reports three long-term survivors off treatment and one euthanized after relapses
- abstract concludes that GS-441524 may achieve long-term resolution of neurologic FIP, with higher doses likely required than for non-neurologic disease

## Neurologic-Treatment Role

This source anchors the high-complexity edge of the FIP treatment branch. Its value is not simply that GS-441524 worked in another set of cats. Its value is that the treated disease form was clinically diagnosed neurologic FIP with CNS involvement, the form most likely to be flattened away if the module only carries a generic antiviral success story.

The abstract-level details are enough to define a separate branch. Four naturally occurring cases were treated for at least 12 weeks at 5-10 mg/kg. Monitoring included neurologic and ophthalmic examinations, and one case included serial MRI, CSF analysis, PCR, and ocular imaging follow-up. All four cats responded, three were alive off treatment, and one was euthanized after relapses.

That combination supports meaningful clinical efficacy signal, but it also preserves complexity. Higher dosage pressure, close monitoring, relapse risk, and tiny sample size must travel with the claim. Neurologic FIP should be modeled as a distinct rescue/high-complexity layer above baseline natural-disease efficacy and beside mixed real-world treatment-package logic.

The safe compiled rule is: neurologic FIP can respond to GS-441524, but this paper does not make neurologic treatment settled, simple, or equivalent to ordinary non-neurologic FIP protocols.

## Limits / Caveats

- current card is deep-extracted at worksheet level, but still not full-text reviewed
- neurologic case interpretation is often more complex than general FIP treatment summaries suggest
- sample size is very small
- relapse in one case blocks clean cure language
- higher-dose logic should not be generalized into protocol advice without full-text and regulatory context
