# One-line Summary

Non-HCM cardiomyopathy review that keeps the HCM module from losing myocardial-disease context.

## Why It Matters For HCM

- helps prevent HCM from becoming the only visible myocardial phenotype in the vault
- may later support cardiomyopathy-boundary pages

## Key Findings

- broader feline cardiomyopathy context source
- non-HCM phenotypes include DCM, RCM, ARVC, LVNC, and NCM
- non-HCM cardiomyopathies are often recognized at heart-failure or thromboembolic presentation rather than in subclinical suspicion
- echocardiography is the confirmatory test for these adjacent cardiomyopathy categories
- useful for disease-boundary handling

## Why This Study Matters

This paper matters because disease modules can become too self-consistent. Once HCM is the main active line of work, there is a tendency for the surrounding cardiomyopathy landscape to disappear from the compiled pages.

This review pushes back against that drift by doing something simple but important: it centers cardiomyopathies other than HCM. The abstract names the adjacent phenotypes that must remain visible: DCM, RCM, ARVC, LVNC, and cardiomyopathy-nonspecific phenotype. It also adds recognition logic. These diseases are rarely suspected in subclinical cats, so they often enter the clinical picture when cats present with heart failure or systemic thromboembolic disease.

That makes it a boundary source, not a core-spine source. Its job is to keep the module honest about what HCM is not, which adjacent phenotypes still matter, and why extension pages are necessary. The echocardiography point is central: adjacent cardiomyopathies are not separated by casual symptom language alone. They require structural confirmation.

That is especially useful for recognition-boundary logic. A page that only names HCM eventually starts sounding as if HCM is the default answer to every feline myocardial question. This paper helps prevent that.
