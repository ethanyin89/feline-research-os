# A feline-focused review of chronic kidney disease-mineral and bone disorders — Part 2: Pathophysiology of calcium disorders and extraosseous calcification

## Evidence-Depth Caveat

This card is based on a full narrative review article integrating current evidence from feline and human CKD-MBD literature. It does not present original quantitative data but synthesizes existing findings and identifies research gaps.

## One-Line Summary

Calcium homeostasis disturbances are central to feline CKD-MBD pathophysiology, with limited direct evidence on serum calcium's effect on nephrocalcinosis/nephrolithiasis; emerging biomarkers FGF23 and α-Klotho show promise but their roles require further elucidation (review of existing studies).

## Why It Matters For Feline ['CKD']

Understanding calcium dysregulation is critical for managing mineral and bone disorders in feline CKD, which may mitigate vascular and soft tissue calcification complications, improve prognostic biomarker use, and guide therapeutic strategies.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

* There is limited evidence characterizing the direct relationship between serum calcium levels and the presence of nephrocalcinosis or nephrolithiasis in cats with CKD.
* Fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23) and α-Klotho have gained attention as biomarkers predicting CKD progression, though their exact impact on calcium homeostasis and ectopic calcification remains unclear.
* Calcium dysregulation contributes to vascular and soft tissue mineralization processes in CKD-MBD documented by human and feline studies.

### source_supported_conclusion

* Dysregulated calcium homeostasis is a fundamental pathological mechanism in feline CKD-MBD contributing to disease progression and vascular/soft tissue calcification.
* Mechanistic insights on FGF23 and α-Klotho from human CKD studies are informative but require validation in feline-specific contexts to understand their role better.

### llm_inference

* There is a need for well-designed, prospective clinical studies in cats with CKD to quantify clinical consequences of calcium disturbances and evaluate biomarker utility (FGF23, α-Klotho) for prognosis and treatment monitoring.
* Improved understanding may offer novel intervention points to reduce extraosseous calcification and improve survival and quality of life in feline CKD patients.
