# A feline-focused review of chronic kidney disease-mineral and bone disorders — Part 2: Pathophysiology of calcium disorders and extraosseous calcification

## One-Line Summary

This feline-focused review shows that feline CKD-MBD is a broader mineral-disorder problem involving calcium dysregulation, FGF23, α-Klotho, and extraosseous calcification, while also warning that clinical consequences and operational use remain incompletely defined.

## Why It Matters For CKD

This is a strong source for expanding the phosphorus/PTH branch beyond first-pass level and for preventing the vault from flattening CKD-MBD into `phosphorus only`.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- Cats with CKD have increased risk of total hypercalcaemia.
- FGF23 increases before the development of azotaemic CKD in cats.
- Dietary phosphate restriction may contribute to hypercalcaemia in some CKD cats.
- The implications of soft tissue calcification in feline CKD remain to be determined.
- Evidence linking serum calcium concentration with nephrocalcinosis and nephrolithiasis is limited.
- Conflicting evidence exists on the role of FGF23 and α-Klotho in calcium homeostasis and vascular and soft tissue calcification.

### source_supported_conclusion

- This source is most relevant to context endpoints and mechanism-adjacent mineral dysregulation.
- Phosphorus still matters centrally, but calcium disorders and calciotropic hormones now need to sit in the same interpretive branch.
- Hypercalcaemia risk should be kept in view when discussing phosphate restriction and CKD-MBD management in cats.
- Extraosseous calcification is better treated as mechanistic context than as a routine clinical endpoint in the current vault.

### llm_inference

- This source justifies a denser CKD-MBD framing and makes a future CKD-MBD subpage more plausible.

## Limits / Caveats

- This is a review, not a feline primary trial.
- The paper itself emphasizes that clinical consequences of calcium disturbances in feline CKD still need prospective study.
- Extraosseous calcification implications remain incompletely defined in cats.
