# Feline CKD: Pathophysiology and risk factors — what do we know?

## One-Line Summary

This review frames feline CKD as a common disorder whose common final outcome is tubulointerstitial fibrosis, while noting that much mechanistic understanding is still extrapolated from human and experimental animal work.

## Why It Matters For CKD

This is a core mechanism-layer source and likely a starting point for disease framing.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- The common final outcome of feline CKD is tubulointerstitial fibrosis.
- Much of the current understanding of CKD pathogenesis is extrapolated from human nephrology and experimental animal studies.
- Major risk factors influencing CKD progression identified over the prior 15 years include proteinuria, plasma phosphate, and plasma creatinine.
- Clinical signs usually occur in later stages of renal disease.
- CKD should be considered in cats with ureterolithiasis, hyperthyroidism, retrovirus infection, systemic hypertension, cardiovascular disease, or urinary tract infection.
- Associated complications that should not be overlooked include systemic hypertension, secondary renal hyperparathyroidism, hypokalaemia, anaemia, and metabolic acidosis.
- The review treats fibrosis as the stable downstream lesion even when upstream causes differ.

### source_supported_conclusion

- Tubulointerstitial fibrosis should remain one of the first mechanistic anchors in the CKD mechanism page.
- The paper is especially valuable because it gives the vault a stable mechanism center without pretending the upstream causal map is already settled in cats.
- Proteinuria, phosphate, and creatinine belong both to mechanism/progression framing and to the endpoint handbook, because this review treats them as progression-linked variables.
- The paper argues for risk-based screening of higher-risk feline populations rather than waiting for late clinical signs.
- The complication list broadens the disease module beyond renal function decline alone and supports cross-links into hypertension, CKD-MBD, potassium, anaemia, and acidosis branches.

### llm_inference

- UGT1A6 may not be a first-wave CKD mechanism card unless later sources tie it directly to feline CKD, so it should remain provisional.
- Fibrosis-first compilation remains safer than cause-first compilation because this review is more decisive about downstream lesion convergence than about initiating mechanisms.

## Why This Review Matters

This source matters because it does two important jobs at once.

First, it provides a stable mechanism backbone. Even if feline CKD begins through multiple different insults, the review keeps returning to one convergent structural outcome: tubulointerstitial fibrosis. That is exactly the kind of anchor a synthesis system needs, because it lets mechanism pages stay stable even when upstream causal certainty is lower.

Second, it links practical recognition pressure to disease biology. If visible clinical signs usually emerge late, then earlier suspicion in high-risk cats is not just a workflow preference. It becomes a disease-structure issue. That is why this review is useful not only for mechanism framing, but also for risk-based recognition and surveillance logic.

The paper is also helpful because it refuses to trap progression variables inside one page family. Proteinuria, phosphate, and creatinine behave here as more than readouts. They help describe how disease worsens. That means they belong in endpoint, mechanism, and translation pages simultaneously.

## Cross-Layer Signal

The safest upgrade this source supports is:

- fibrosis is the most stable mechanism backbone
- progression variables should be treated as cross-layer objects
- symptom timing justifies risk-based screening
- complication burden is broader than renal dysfunction alone

That combination is exactly why this card should not stay a thin review stub.

## Limits / Caveats

- This is a review-level source, not a primary mechanistic study.
- Some causal framing may reflect extrapolation rather than cat-specific proof.
- The source is strongest for broad disease framing and risk structure, not for ranking specific upstream mediators or assigning intervention efficacy.
