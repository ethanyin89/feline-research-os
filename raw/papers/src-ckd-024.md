# Renal biomarkers in cats: A review of the current status in chronic kidney disease

## One-Line Summary

This biomarker review shows why classical renal markers are insufficient for early feline CKD detection, supports SDMA as a useful GFR biomarker with still-limited specificity data, and broadens the biomarker map beyond SDMA toward tubular, glomerular, and fibrosis-related markers.

## Why It Matters For CKD

This is one of the strongest new sources for densifying the endpoint, early-detection, and SDMA-positioning branches.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- Serum creatinine is the classical biomarker of CKD in cats, but has important limitations that reduce its value as a biomarker of early CKD.
- Serum SDMA was introduced as a novel glomerular filtration rate biomarker for early detection of CKD in cats.
- Data on SDMA specificity are still limited.
- Novel renal biomarkers are being pursued to detect glomerular or tubular dysfunction earlier than conventional biomarkers.
- Changes in serum or urinary biomarker concentrations may indicate early kidney damage or predict CKD progression before conventional biomarkers change.
- The review states that knowledge on renal biomarkers in feline CKD has progressed substantially over the last 5 years.
- UPC is a routine renal biomarker and persistent renal proteinuria without azotemia may indicate early glomerular disease in cats.
- UPC specificity is reduced by non-renal diseases such as hyperthyroidism, viral infections, and lower urinary tract disease.
- Direct GFR measurement remains the gold standard and most sensitive test for impaired renal function, but is still mostly a research tool rather than a routine clinical method.
- The review expands the biomarker map beyond SDMA toward glomerular, tubular, fibrosis-related, and progression-related injury markers.

### source_supported_conclusion

- This review strengthens the current vault position that SDMA matters, but should not be treated as a finished answer to early detection on its own.
- The biomarker landscape in feline CKD is broader than the current first-wave endpoint shortlist, but many newer markers still sit closer to research-stage than routine operational use.
- Creatinine, USG, UPC/proteinuria, and SDMA should continue to be interpreted as serving different roles rather than being collapsed into one renal-severity scale.
- UPC/proteinuria remains important, but this review reinforces that its specificity is context-dependent and can be degraded by non-renal disease.
- The review supports keeping GFR as a reference-standard concept rather than promoting it into the routine working endpoint core.
- The review also supports keeping investigational biomarkers below the routine first-wave endpoint layer until extraction and clinical-usability evidence are stronger.

### llm_inference

- This source reduces the uncertainty around whether SDMA belongs in the map at all. It does.
- The remaining uncertainty is more specific: how strongly SDMA should be weighted against serial creatinine, USG, and the broader surveillance workflow.
- This paper is a strong candidate for later deep extraction because it touches several endpoint branches at once: SDMA, creatinine, UPC, GFR, and newer injury biomarkers.

## Why This Review Matters

This source is valuable because it reduces a recurring endpoint error:

- treating SDMA as either the answer to early detection
- or as too uncertain to matter at all

The review supports neither extreme.

The safer read is:

- creatinine still matters
- SDMA matters
- GFR remains ideal but impractical
- UPC is useful but context-sensitive
- newer biomarkers expand the field without replacing the current practical core

That is exactly the kind of bounded upgrade the gap analysis needs.
