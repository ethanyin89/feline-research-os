# Clinical management of feline chronic kidney disease in Portugal: a questionnaire-based study

## One-Line Summary

This questionnaire study shows how feline CKD is actually being managed in Portuguese practice and, more importantly, where guideline awareness still fails to translate into consistent blood-pressure measurement, diet implementation, and follow-up.

## Why It Matters For CKD

This is useful for the translation layer because it exposes implementation gaps between what clinicians know, what they do, and what owners can realistically sustain.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- 409 veterinary practitioners responded, with 53.5% diagnosing 2-5 feline CKD cases per month.
- 92.7% reported using IRIS guidelines, but only 19.1% systematically measured SBP in all CKD cats.
- 99.3% advised a renal diet, but many reported that it represented less than 75% of daily intake for most patients.
- Renal diet was often prescribed regardless of stage and without proper gradual transition.
- Appetite stimulants, calcium channel blockers, ACE inhibitors, and phosphate binders were frequently prescribed.
- Only 35.7% were able to comply with recommended stable-patient reassessment periodicity because of owner constraints.

### source_supported_conclusion

- This paper is useful for practice-pattern context and implementation realism, not as a mechanistic or efficacy source.
- The study supports the idea that hypertension substaging is under-executed in practice even when guideline awareness is high.
- The paper reinforces renal diet as dominant practice, but also shows that implementation quality and owner adherence are limiting realities.
- This source is strong support for the recurring vault caution that common use does not equal proven value.

### llm_inference

- This should strengthen the `evidence vs implementation gap` theme in the translation layer.

## Limits / Caveats

- Questionnaire-based studies are useful for behavior mapping, but usually weaker for efficacy claims.
- This is Portugal-specific practice data and may not generalize directly to every market.
- Reported behavior may differ from real recorded practice behavior.
