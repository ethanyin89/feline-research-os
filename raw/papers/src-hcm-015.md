# One-line Summary

Treatment-skepticism source that should stop the HCM translation branch from being flattened into optimism or consensus theater.

## Why It Matters For HCM

- makes evidence quality itself part of the module
- may become the key anti-overclaim anchor in treatment interpretation
- now gives the HCM shell a safer rule that common prescribing behavior is not the same as strong evidence

## Key Findings

- explicit science-versus-faith framing
- abstract directly reports a mismatch between therapeutic decision-making and documented evidence support
- survey-plus-literature-search design makes it a boundary paper, not just an opinion piece

## Why This Study Matters

This study matters because it gives the HCM treatment branch an internal control mechanism.

Many disease modules drift into a simple mistake: if a therapy is commonly prescribed, the compiled page starts sounding as though the evidence hierarchy is already known. This paper directly resists that drift. It compares clinician choices with published support and shows that the two do not line up cleanly.

That makes the source much more than a provocative title. It is a structural warning that treatment evidence quality must be modeled as part of the branch itself. In HCM, this matters especially because symptomatic goals, thromboembolism prevention, and delay of progression all sound rational, but rational goals do not automatically mean strong supporting evidence.

## Treatment-Skepticism Signal

The safest promotion from this source is:

- treatment goals can be sensible while evidence support remains weak
- clinician consensus should not be confused with documented proof
- treatment-evidence quality deserves its own place in HCM translation
- later targeted-therapy enthusiasm should still be read against this older skepticism anchor

That is why this card belongs near the center of the translation branch rather than at its edge.

It also gives the module a cleaner way to compare eras. Older conventional therapy papers, newer targeted-frontier papers, and broad management reviews can all coexist, but they should be read through this filter: practical use, mechanistic plausibility, and documented support are related questions, not interchangeable ones.

That distinction matters because it prevents the translation layer from treating frequency of use, confidence of rationale, and strength of proof as if they were the same evidentiary signal.

## Limits / Caveats

- current card is abstract-weighted, not full-text reviewed
- The source is strongest for evidence-quality boundary setting, not for ranking every therapy individually from abstract-level access alone.
