# One-line Summary

Older enalapril treatment study that adds a conventional-therapy layer to the HCM translation branch.

## Why It Matters For HCM

- prevents the treatment branch from looking purely modern and targeted
- may help show how weak or uneven older intervention evidence is

## Key Findings

- dedicated treatment study
- conventional therapy anchor
- retrospective 19-cat HCM cohort
- CHF count improved from 11 at prescription to one at 3-6 month reexamination in the abstract
- no reported adverse effects on blood pressure, creatinine, or potassium in the abstract
- authors explicitly called for controlled prospective studies

## Why This Study Matters

This paper matters because it keeps the HCM treatment branch from looking artificially modern. Without a source like this, compiled translation pages can drift into a false contrast between only two choices: current bounded management language and targeted-future enthusiasm.

Enalapril gives the branch a visible older conventional-treatment layer. The abstract adds enough detail to make the paper useful, but not enough to make it decisive. Nineteen cats were evaluated retrospectively. Eleven were in congestive heart failure when enalapril was prescribed, and only one was in CHF when cats were reexamined three to six months later. The abstract also reports echocardiographic changes in cardiac dimensions and no noted adverse effects on blood pressure, serum creatinine, or potassium.

Those observations are clinically interesting, but their main value in the wiki is still structural. This is not just another intervention citation. It is a marker of an older therapeutic logic that should remain visible as historical context rather than being silently merged with newer mechanism-linked treatment thinking.

That is also why this source sits naturally beside the skepticism branch. It helps explain why HCM treatment hierarchy remains unsettled: different eras, different intervention logics, and different evidence standards are all mixed together inside the literature.

The abstract's own final sentence is the controlling boundary. The authors did not treat the retrospective signal as proof. They said controlled prospective studies were needed. That makes the paper useful for two simultaneous claims: enalapril appeared tolerable and potentially associated with improvement in that cohort, and efficacy was still unproven. The translation layer should preserve both halves.

## Limits / Caveats

- abstract-weighted: retrospective design and 19-cat sample size are noted in the abstract; effect size and comparator data require full-text review
- 1998 publication date: predates modern genetics, echocardiographic staging, and evidence-based HCM management frameworks
- the abstract itself calls for controlled prospective studies, so efficacy is not established by this paper alone
- this card supports historical bounding and branch-width more strongly than any outcome-size interpretation
- without a control group, CHF-status improvement cannot be attributed cleanly to enalapril
