# Systematic review of feline diabetic remission: Separating fact from opinion

## One-Line Summary

Systematic review anchor for deciding which feline diabetic remission claims are evidence-backed.

## Why It Matters For Feline Diabetes

- remission is one of the most strategically important and easiest-to-overstate topics in feline diabetes
- this source should control the boundary between treatment optimism and evidence

## Key Findings

- abstract-level extraction confirms this as the remission boundary anchor
- systematic review included 22 studies and used established bias/evidence assessment tools
- abstract identifies frequent weaknesses: lack of randomization/blinding, small samples, missing diagnostic/remission criteria, and poor confounder control
- abstract reports no single remission predictor and notes documented remission across multiple insulin types and protocols
- the key clinical value is not a remission-rate number; it is the discipline to separate `remission possible` from `remission predictable`
- carbohydrate reduction can remain visible as a possible beneficial factor, but not as an isolated remission guarantee from this card alone
- the paper's title and abstract make evidence hygiene part of the finding: fact/opinion separation is the operating rule

## Limits / Caveats

- extraction is abstract-weighted, not full-text
- exact included studies, remission definitions, and bias judgments are not captured yet
- do not promote exact remission-rate comparisons from this card
- do not convert remission evidence into a protocol leaderboard without full-text definitions and design checks
- do not treat non-insulin-dependent status, insulin-dose reduction, and rigorously defined remission as automatically interchangeable endpoints

## Remission Boundary Logic

This source is the best current control point for diabetes pages because it supports both sides of the remission story.

What can be promoted:

- remission is possible in cats
- remission belongs in the endpoint and treatment architecture
- treatment, diet, and insulin choices may interact with remission
- remission should be tracked separately from short-term glycemic improvement

What should be held:

- one-factor prediction
- one diet rule
- one insulin-type winner
- remission-rate comparisons across studies without checking definitions
- any implication that remission potential erases chronic beta-cell, obesity, endocrine, pancreatitis, or monitoring constraints
