# Prevalence of Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy and ALMS1 Variant in Sphynx Cats in New Zealand

## Evidence-Depth Caveat

This card is based on the complete publication text and is deep-extracted as a clinical study.

## One-Line Summary

In a cohort of 55 apparently healthy Sphynx cats in New Zealand, 40% were diagnosed with HCM by a cardiologist at median age 5.8 years; 70.9% carried the ALMS1 variant, which showed no association with HCM.

## Why It Matters For Feline ['HCM']

Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy is a common cardiac disorder in cats with significant morbidity and mortality. Demonstrating a high prevalence in Sphynx cats underscores the need for routine cardiac screening in this breed. The lack of association with the ALMS1 variant challenges previous assumptions, guiding clinicians towards more comprehensive diagnostic approaches beyond genetic testing, improving disease surveillance and management.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

* 55 Sphynx cats were prospectively screened; 42 had repeat exams after a median interval of 1.8 years (range 1.6–2.2).
* HCM prevalence was 40% (20/55 cats) at median 5.8 years (range 2.4–13.1).
* ALMS1 variant was present in 70.9% of cats: 11 homozygous and 28 heterozygous carriers.
* Three cats with HCM died during the study with congestive heart failure; necropsies revealed focal but extensive myocardial ischemia or infarction.
* No statistically significant association between ALMS1 variant status and HCM diagnosis was found.

### source_supported_conclusion

* Sphynx cats in New Zealand show a high predisposition to HCM.
* The ALMS1 variant, despite high prevalence, is not predictive of HCM in this population.
* Congestive heart failure with ischemic myocardial injury may represent a pathological consequence of advanced HCM in this breed.

### llm_inference

* Regular cardiologist screening for HCM is advisable for Sphynx cats due to their significant risk.
* Genetic testing for ALMS1 variant alone should not be used to screen for HCM risk.
* Longitudinal monitoring is warranted to detect progression and enable early intervention.
