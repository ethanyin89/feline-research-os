# ISFM Consensus Guidelines on the Diagnosis and Management of Feline Chronic Kidney Disease

## One-Line Summary

ISFM guideline positions feline CKD as a common, progressive disease requiring repeated reassessment, with diagnosis and staging built around sustained renal dysfunction plus urinalysis, biochemistry, blood pressure, imaging, and cautious use of advanced tests such as SDMA and GFR.

## Why It Matters For CKD

This is likely the highest-priority practice guideline in the current seed corpus.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- Older cats older than 7 years are recommended to receive health checks every 6 months, with at least annual selected diagnostic testing.
- In practice, feline CKD is often diagnosed using increased serum creatinine above 140 µmol/l, an inappropriately low USG below 1.035, and evidence that these abnormalities are sustained.
- A persistent increase in serum creatinine greater than 15% from a prior baseline can also indicate reduced renal function.
- Historical or clinical findings such as weight loss, altered kidney size, unexplained dehydration, PU/PD, hypertension, or unexplained low USG justify further workup even before severe azotaemia is obvious.
- The minimum routine database includes urinalysis with UPCR, serum biochemistry, haematology, systolic blood pressure, and diagnostic imaging.
- The purpose of the minimum routine database is not only diagnosis, but also identifying underlying aetiologies, CKD complications, and concomitant disease that may affect management.
- IRIS staging is based on fasting creatinine in stable, hydrated cats, with substaging based on UPCR and systolic blood pressure.
- GFR remains the gold standard for renal function assessment, but practical limitations keep it outside routine first-line use.
- SDMA appears more sensitive than creatinine for early CKD detection and is less influenced by muscle mass, but the guideline does not endorse it as a standalone screening shortcut.
- Renal diets are reported to reduce clinical signs of uraemia and significantly prolong longevity in cats with CKD.
- The guideline cites survival comparisons of 633 vs 264 days and 480 vs 210 days in favor of renal diets over maintenance diets.
- Phosphate restriction is treated as a major component of dietary benefit, not a side note to diet selection.
- Intestinal phosphate binders become important when diet alone is insufficient and should be split through the day with food.
- The guideline provides concrete phosphate-binder dose ranges, including aluminium hydroxide or carbonate 90 mg/kg, calcium carbonate 90 mg/kg, calcium acetate 60-90 mg/kg, sevelamer 90-160 mg/kg, and lanthanum 30-90 mg/kg.

### source_supported_conclusion

- This guideline should be treated as the baseline operational source for CKD diagnosis, staging, monitoring, and treatment prioritisation.
- Creatinine, USG, UPCR, and systolic blood pressure belong in the first-pass endpoint handbook because the guideline treats them as core clinical decision variables.
- The guideline clearly separates routine diagnosis from earlier or non-azotaemic CKD detection, so the endpoint page should distinguish core diagnosis from early-detection support.
- This source strengthens the vault-wide view that early detection in feline CKD is a serial-surveillance problem, not a one-off screening problem.
- The guideline supports SDMA as an adjunctive early-detection and staging aid, while explicitly rejecting it as a standalone screening shortcut.
- The treatment layer should continue to rank renal diet and phosphorus control unusually high, because the guideline gives both operational priority and stronger evidence footing than many adjunct interventions.
