# Feline Oral Squamous Cell Carcinoma: A Critical Review of Etiologic Factors

## Evidence-Depth Caveat

This card has abstract-level extraction from PubMed (PMID: 36288171). It is a 2022 PRISMA-compliant systematic review of FOSCC etiology. Full-text deep extraction is recommended before controlling the oral SCC branch.

## One-Line Summary

Systematic review of FOSCC etiology covering papillomavirus, tobacco smoke, diet, and oral comorbidities as potential risk factors.

## Why It Matters For Feline Cancer

This source provides the first systematic review of oral SCC etiologic factors for the cancer module. It can anchor the oral SCC branch etiology discussion once deep-extracted.

Key abstract findings:

- FOSCC is the most common oral neoplasia in cats
- Locally invasive with high mortality, etiology unknown
- 26 studies included from 553 publications (2000-2022)
- Papillomavirus detected in 16.2% of FOSCC samples
- 35.2% of FOSCC cats had tobacco smoke exposure history
- Canned food: 4.7x increased risk (one study)
- Deworming collars: 5.3x increased risk (one study)
- 6.4% had concurrent oral pathology

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- "Feline oral squamous cell carcinoma (FOSCC) is the most common oral neoplasia in cats."
- "This malignant tumor is locally invasive, has a high mortality rate, and its etiology is not yet known."
- "When evaluated, feline papillomavirus was detected in 16.2% of samples of FOSCC."
- "In the three studies focused on exposure to tobacco smoke, 35.2% (30/85) of cats with FOSCC had a history of this exposure."
- "The consumption of canned food and the use of deworming collars were associated, in only one publication, with a risk of neoplasia increased by 4.7 and 5.3 times, respectively."
