# Feline Oral Squamous Cell Carcinoma: A Critical Review of Etiologic Factors

## Evidence-Depth Caveat

**Deep-extracted from PMC full text (PMC9609408).** This is a 2022 PRISMA-compliant systematic review of FOSCC etiology. Evidence level: systematic review. Full text verified via Europe PMC XML.

## One-Line Summary

Systematic review of FOSCC etiology (26 studies, 669 cats) covering papillomavirus, tobacco smoke, diet, and oral comorbidities as potential risk factors.

## Why It Matters For Feline Cancer

This source provides the most comprehensive systematic review of oral SCC etiologic factors for the cancer module. It anchors the oral SCC branch etiology discussion with quantified evidence across multiple risk factor categories.

Key findings from full-text extraction:

- **FOSCC epidemiology:** Most common oral neoplasia in cats; locally invasive; high mortality; etiology unclear
- **Systematic search:** 628 publications screened → 26 studies included (2000-2022)
- **Total population:** 669 cats with oral SCC evaluated
- **Patient demographics:** Ages 2-20 years; 258 females/233 males (n=437 with data)

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

**Papillomavirus:**
- "PV being detected in 43 cases (14.7%) across 14 studies evaluating 292 FOSCC cases"
- "FcaPV-2 is the most prevalent type"
- "The incidence of PV ranged between 0 and 100%" (methodology-dependent)
- Detection methods: PCR, IHC, ISH, ViroCap

**Tobacco Smoke:**
- "35.2% (30/85) of cats with FOSCC had a history of tobacco smoke exposure"
- "The risk of developing oral SCC was two times higher in cats exposed to ETS when compared to those never exposed"
- "A tendency for the rise in the expression of p53 (4.5 times) in cats exposed to ETS was observed (p=0.19)"
- "Neither of these studies found a statistically significant correlation between ETS and the development of the tumor"

**Dietary Factors:**
- "About 58.5% of these animals ate canned food, 56.6% canned tuna, and only 22% ate dry diet"
- "The consumption of canned food was associated with a risk of neoplasia increased by 4.7 times"
- "The use of deworming collars was associated with a risk of neoplasia increased by 5.3 times"

**Other Factors:**
- "Among 485 cats with FOSCC, 6.4% had dental and oral pathology"
- FIV, FeLV: limited investigation, low incidence reported
- EBV: 1/20 positive (coinfected with FcaPV-3)

**Methodology:**
- "628 references obtained in initial search on PubMed/MEDLINE"
- "Only 26 original studies were included in this critical review"
- "The selected studies evaluated 669 cats with oral SCC"
- "Sample sizes ranged between 1 and 113 individuals/samples"

### source_supported_conclusion

- FOSCC etiology is multifactorial with no single dominant cause established
- Papillomavirus involvement is present but inconsistent (14.7% average, 0-100% range)
- Tobacco smoke shows trends (2x risk, p53 elevation) but lacks statistical significance
- Diet factors (canned food, tuna) are based on single publications only
- FOSCC serves as spontaneous model for human HNSCC (comparative oncology value)
- Evidence quality is limited by small study sizes, methodological variability, and retrospective designs

### llm_inference

- Risk prevention advice should include broad caveats about evidence uncertainty
- Papillomavirus vaccination research may be warranted despite low overall detection
- Tobacco avoidance recommendation is reasonable despite statistical limitations (precautionary principle)
- Diet factor claims should not be promoted to owner-facing guidance without replication

## Comparative Oncology Value

This review establishes FOSCC as a spontaneous model for human head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC):

- Similar risk factors (tobacco, HPV parallels with PV)
- p53/TP53 pathway involvement
- Local invasiveness and poor prognosis
- HNSCC is the sixth most common human cancer
