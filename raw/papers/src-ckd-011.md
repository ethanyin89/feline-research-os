# Renal fibrosis in feline chronic kidney disease: Known mediators and mechanisms of injury

## One-Line Summary

This review argues that renal tubulointerstitial fibrosis is the dominant pathological lesion in feline CKD and a plausible therapeutic target, while explicitly warning that only a small subset of the broader fibrosis-mediator literature has been investigated directly in cats.

## Why It Matters For CKD

This is likely one of the most important mechanism-layer sources for injury pathway mapping.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- In most cases of feline CKD, the underlying aetiology is unknown, but the most frequently reported pathological diagnosis is renal tubulointerstitial fibrosis.
- Renal fibrosis is characterized by extensive accumulation of extracellular matrix within the interstitium.
- Renal fibrosis is described as the final common pathway for kidney diseases and the pathological lesion best correlated with function in both humans and cats.
- As a convergent pathway, renal fibrosis provides an ideal target for treatment of CKD and understanding the fibrotic process is described as essential for future development of novel therapies.
- The review states that many mediators and mechanisms of renal fibrosis are reported in the literature, but only a few have been investigated in the cat.
- Keywords highlighted for the review include phosphate, proteinuria, and TGF-beta.
- The paper shifts the mechanistic focus away from uncertain initiating causes and toward the most stable shared lesion-level answer in feline CKD.

### source_supported_conclusion

- Renal fibrosis is no longer just a candidate mechanism, it is the clearest current backbone for the feline CKD mechanism page.
- Proteinuria and phosphate should be treated as mechanistically relevant progression-linked variables, not just treatment-monitoring markers.
- This source justifies adding a fibrosis-focused substructure under mechanism before expanding into broader species-difference topics.
- This paper also justifies keeping the mediator layer cautious: TGF-beta is a strong next-step target for extraction, but not yet a fully validated feline CKD intervention target in the current vault.
- This source supports treating fibrosis as a translational target candidate while still preserving the gap between mechanistic plausibility and feline intervention proof.

### llm_inference

- TGF-beta is a stronger first-wave mechanistic expansion target than UGT1A6 for CKD-specific work, because this paper explicitly centers fibrosis mediators.
- The vault should continue to treat fibrosis as the mechanism anchor and TGF-beta as the first serious mediator-level expansion, while resisting the temptation to over-import broader non-feline fibrosis literature.

## Why This Review Matters

This source is important because it solves a recurrent CKD knowledge problem.

If the vault organizes feline CKD around initiating causes, it stays thin because the cause is still unknown in many cats.
If the vault organizes around the most reproducible lesion-level convergence, it becomes much more stable.

This review clearly pushes the second option:

- fibrosis is the most frequently reported lesion
- fibrosis best tracks function
- fibrosis is therefore the safest mechanism anchor

## Mechanism Write-Back Signal

The main upgrades from this paper are:

- keep fibrosis as the first mechanism backbone
- keep phosphorus and proteinuria visible inside the fibrosis branch
- treat TGF-beta as a serious mediator watchlist target
- preserve explicit caution that the mediator literature is only partly cat-validated

That makes this source foundational for mechanism pages, but still not a license to overstate anti-fibrotic intervention readiness.

## Limits / Caveats

- This is a review source, not a feline interventional study.
- The current extraction still does not rank which individual mediators have the strongest cat-specific evidence.
- The source is strongest for lesion-backbone and evidence-ceiling logic, not for detailed mediator prioritization or treatment efficacy claims.
