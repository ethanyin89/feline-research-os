# Feline chronic kidney disease is associated with shortened telomeres and increased cellular senescence

## One-Line Summary

This primary study shows that naturally occurring feline CKD is associated with kidney-specific telomere shortening and increased cellular senescence, which strengthens the aged-cat mechanism branch without replacing fibrosis as the main disease backbone.

## Why It Matters For CKD

This is useful for deeper mechanism work because it upgrades senescence from speculative aging language to a real feline kidney-level finding, while still sitting below fibrosis in current practical importance.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- Kidney, liver, and skin samples were evaluated from 12 CKD cats, 12 young normal cats, and 6 old normal cats.
- Proximal tubular epithelial cells in CKD kidneys had significantly shorter telomeres than those in young and geriatric normal cats.
- Distal tubular epithelial cells in CKD kidneys had significantly shorter telomeres than those in young normal cats.
- Senescence-associated beta-galactosidase staining was significantly increased in CKD kidneys compared with young normal cats.
- No significant telomere shortening was found in liver or skin.
- Renal telomerase activity remained comparably low across groups.

### source_supported_conclusion

- This source strengthens mechanism richness and specifically supports a renal senescence branch in naturally occurring feline CKD.
- The finding is kidney-localized in this study rather than a general organism-wide aging readout.
- The paper supports a future senescence-target discussion, but not a change to the V1 operational backbone.

### llm_inference

- If the vault later adds an aging-pathogenesis or senolytic-target branch, this becomes a key anchor source.

## Limits / Caveats

- Cellular senescence findings may be mechanistically interesting without yet changing clinical workflow.
- Group sizes were still modest.
- Increased SABG staining versus geriatric normals did not reach statistical significance in one comparison.
- The study is association-rich but does not prove that telomere shortening is the initiating driver of feline CKD.
