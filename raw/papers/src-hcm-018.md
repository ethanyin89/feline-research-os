# One-line Summary

Retrospective broader-cardiomyopathy study that can add non-HCM context around the HCM module.

## Why It Matters For HCM

- helps keep disease boundaries honest
- may later support extension or classification pages

## Key Findings

- retrospective cardiomyopathy context
- not HCM-only framing
- 106-cat idiopathic cardiomyopathy cohort from 1994-2001
- HCM majority signal, but RCM/DCM/UCM remain substantial
- median follow-up survival context available for 73 cats

## Why This Study Matters

This paper matters because broader retrospective cardiomyopathy material plays a different role from HCM-specific reviews. Its value is not precision inside the HCM spine. Its value is boundary honesty.

A module can become internally coherent while still being externally misleading. That happens when HCM pages gradually absorb the whole feline cardiomyopathy landscape and stop signaling what lies outside their main disease frame. This retrospective idiopathic-cardiomyopathy study helps resist that drift by keeping a wider cohort and a wider disease label visible.

The abstract provides a useful historical distribution anchor. Among 106 cats with idiopathic cardiomyopathy, HCM was the largest category at 57.5%, but RCM, DCM, and UCM were also represented. That means HCM can be described as dominant in the cohort without becoming synonymous with feline cardiomyopathy. The survival information also matters: the median survival time for cats with follow-up data was 300 days, and survival differed by subtype, with UCM longer than HCM, RCM, or DCM in the abstract.

That means the safest use of this source is not to sharpen HCM phenotype detail. It is to keep extension, classification, and boundary pages honest about the fact that HCM lives inside a bigger feline cardiomyopathy map.

## Limits / Caveats

- abstract-weighted: the 57.5% HCM proportion and 300-day median survival are abstract-backed; subtype-specific outcome data and study inclusion details require full-text review
- this card currently supports boundary framing more strongly than subtype distribution claims
- historical cohort from 1994-2001 may not map cleanly to current diagnostic classification or management
- retrospective referral-center context can distort apparent frequency and survival
