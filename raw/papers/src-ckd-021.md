# Aldosterone and the mineralocorticoid receptor in renal injury: A potential therapeutic target in feline chronic kidney disease

## One-Line Summary

This review expands the mediator and therapeutic-target layer beyond TGF-beta by positioning aldosterone/MR signaling as a plausible renal-injury driver and translational target in feline CKD, but with much stronger support from non-feline experimental and human evidence than from direct feline efficacy data.

## Why It Matters For CKD

This is useful because it builds a richer mediator-to-therapy branch and stops the vault from treating RAAS logic as ACEI/ARB-only biology.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

- MR activation is supported as a powerful mediator of renal damage in laboratory animals and humans.
- The strongest proposed mechanisms include aldosterone-induced vasculopathy, oxidative stress, inflammation, and growth-factor signaling that promotes fibroblast proliferation and extracellular-matrix dysregulation.
- MR antagonists such as spironolactone and eplerenone abrogate renal injury in animal models, including ischaemia-induced damage.
- Human CKD trials show benefit on proteinuria and cardiovascular events, but not yet on mortality or CKD-progression endpoints.
- Feline CKD shares important characteristics with human disease including tubulointerstitial fibrosis.
- In experimentally induced feline CKD after ischaemia/reperfusion injury, plasma renin, aldosterone, angiotensin I, and angiotensin II increase.
- In naturally occurring normotensive azotaemic feline CKD, plasma renin activity and aldosterone concentrations may not differ from age-matched controls.

### source_supported_conclusion

- This source belongs in mechanism-to-translation expansion, not in the first-wave operational core.
- Aldosterone/MR signaling is now a stronger watchlist mediator branch in the vault.
- The paper supports MR antagonism as a plausible therapeutic direction, but not as a clinically validated feline CKD treatment class.
- The mixed feline signal means aldosterone biology should be treated cautiously in naturally occurring disease interpretation.

### llm_inference

- This can now be treated as a second-generation mechanism target branch after fibrosis, TGF-beta, and the mineral branch.

## Limits / Caveats

- Therapeutic-target papers can overpromise relative to current clinical evidence.
- Much of the strongest supporting evidence comes from laboratory species and human CKD.
- Feline naturally occurring CKD evidence around aldosterone is more mixed than the broad translational story.
- The review itself does not justify strong feline mortality or progression-slowing claims for MR antagonists.

## Open Follow-Up Questions

- Is there enough later feline evidence to justify an aldosterone/MR entity page now?
- Which feline studies best distinguish RAAS activation in experimental versus naturally occurring CKD?
- Does MR antagonism meaningfully improve proteinuria or structural outcomes in cats beyond general theoretical plausibility?
