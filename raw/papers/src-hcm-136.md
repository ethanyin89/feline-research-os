# Cardiac troponin I in healthy Norwegian Forest Cat, Birman and domestic shorthair cats, and in cats with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy

## Evidence-Depth Caveat

This card is based on complete publication text. It is deep-extracted as a clinical study.

## One-Line Summary

In a cohort of 96 healthy cats and 39 cats with HCM, serum cTnI concentrations were influenced by breed and sex in healthy cats, and significantly elevated correlating with echocardiographic severity markers in cats with HCM (P values as low as 0.0003).

## Why It Matters For Feline ['HCM']

Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy is a common cardiac disease in cats with variable clinical presentations. Identifying reliable biomarkers like cTnI that correlate with disease presence and severity can aid earlier diagnosis, monitoring progression, and therapeutic decision-making. Understanding physiological influences such as breed and sex on baseline cTnI improves diagnostic accuracy.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

* Healthy cohort included 33 Norwegian Forest Cat, 33 Birman, and 30 domestic shorthair cats; HCM cohort comprised 39 cats.
* Birman cats had significantly higher cTnI than Norwegian Forest Cats (P = 0.014).
* Neutered males showed higher cTnI levels than intact females (P = 0.032).
* cTnI positively correlated with heart rate in healthy cats (P < 0.0001).
* In HCM cats, cTnI was positively associated with left ventricular wall thickness and left atrial-to-aortic root ratio (all P ≤ 0.010).
* Cats with HCM had significantly higher cTnI than healthy cats; within HCM group, those with left atrial enlargement had higher cTnI (P = 0.0003).

### source_supported_conclusion

* Breed and sex differences should be accounted for when interpreting cTnI in healthy cats.
* Serum cTnI concentration reflects myocardial injury or stress severity and correlates with echocardiographic markers of HCM severity.
* Elevated serum cTnI can differentiate cats with HCM (especially with LAE) from healthy individuals.

### llm_inference

* Measurement of serum cTnI may improve detection and monitoring of HCM in clinical practice.
* Breed- and sex-specific reference ranges for cTnI could reduce false positives/negatives in diagnosis.
* Rising cTnI in individual cats over time might indicate disease progression warranting increased clinical attention.
