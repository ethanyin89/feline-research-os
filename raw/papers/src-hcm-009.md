# One-line Summary

Foundational echocardiographic phenotype paper for HCM recognition and endpoint architecture, with real evidence that spontaneous feline HCM is morphologically heterogeneous.

## Why It Matters For HCM

- likely anchors structural phenotype recognition
- also connects phenotype assessment to the comparative model branch
- now gives the HCM shell a safer first-pass rule that echo-led phenotype definition should outrank biomarker-only logic

## Key Findings

- echocardiographic assessment focus
- spontaneous disease framing
- abstract followed 46 cats and reports substantial event burden including heart failure, embolization, or sudden death during follow-up
- abstract reports both diffuse and segmental patterns of hypertrophy rather than one uniform shape

## Why This Study Matters

This study matters because it gives the HCM module a direct structural phenotype anchor rather than forcing the page layer to infer recognition authority from broader reviews.

The strongest signal here is that echocardiography is not just one test among many. It is the method used to define the phenotype architecture of spontaneous feline disease. That matters because the HCM module keeps facing pressure from biomarkers and newer computational approaches. This paper helps hold the hierarchy in place: structure comes first.

The second important gain is heterogeneity control. The study does not describe one flat hypertrophy pattern. It reports a small left-ventricular cavity with variable hypertrophy patterns, usually diffuse but sometimes segmental. That means the recognition branch should not collapse feline HCM into a single canonical morphologic picture.

The third gain is consequence linkage. This is not only an imaging description paper. The source card captures meaningful follow-up event burden, including heart failure, embolization, and sudden death. That gives the structural phenotype branch real clinical weight.

## Structural Recognition Signal

The safest promotion from this source is:

- echocardiography leads operational phenotype definition
