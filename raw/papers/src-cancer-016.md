# Tumor microenvironment of human breast cancer, and feline mammary carcinoma as a potential study model

## Evidence-Depth Caveat

This card is based on the complete publication text. It is deep-extracted as a comparative oncological review.

## One-Line Summary

A 2021 review detailing the tumor microenvironment (TME) components in breast cancer and validating feline mammary carcinoma (FMC) as a comparative study model.

## Why It Matters For Feline Cancer

This study establishes the similarity of the tumor microenvironment between human breast cancer and feline mammary carcinoma (FMC). By confirming that FMC shares key clinicopathological, histopathological, and immunological pathways, it supports using the cat as a model for breast cancer while laying the groundwork for TME-targeted immunotherapies in cats.

## Key Findings

### quoted_fact

* "FMC has been proposed as a reliable cancer model for the study of human breast cancer, as they share clinicopathological, histopathological and epidemiological features, as well as the pathways involved in cancer initiation and progression."
* "The tumor microenvironment (TME) is composed of distinct cellular and non-cellular elements, including tumor infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), natural killer cells, tumor infiltrating dendritic cells (TIDCs), tumor associated macrophages (TAMs), tumor associated neutrophils (TANs), cancer associated fibroblasts (CAFs), myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), distinct pro-angiogenic factors and immune checkpoint biomarkers."

### source_supported_conclusion

* Investigating the immune cell infiltrate within the FMC microenvironment is a prerequisite for selecting and developing effective immunotherapeutic regimens.
* The overlap in pathways involved in tumor initiation and progression makes therapeutic findings in one species highly relevant to the other.

### llm_inference

* Since CAFs and TAMs are key orchestrators of immune escape in human breast cancer, targeting these microenvironmental components in cats could prevent metastases in aggressive FMC cases.
