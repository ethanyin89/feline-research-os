# src-ibd-010 Deep Extraction Round 1

## Core Takeaway

`muscularis thickening is a lymphoma-leaning imaging signal, not a stand-alone diagnosis`

## What This Source Most Strongly Adds

- the first real imaging anchor in the IBD-versus-lymphoma boundary branch
- support for treating ultrasound as a meaningful suspicion-shaping layer
- a reason to separate `lymphadenopathy` from `muscularis thickening` instead of flattening both into one imaging label

## Quoted / Near-Quoted Facts

- The abstract reports 142 cats with histologic diagnosis of normal small intestine, lymphoma, or IBD.
- Cats with muscularis propria thickening were more likely to have lymphoma compared with normal small intestine cats and compared with IBD cats.
- The abstract reports an odds ratio of 18.8 for lymphoma versus IBD when muscularis propria thickening was present.
- Cats with ultrasonographic evidence of lymphadenopathy were more likely to have lymphoma or IBD than normal small intestine.
- The abstract concludes that older cats with muscularis layer thickening are more likely to have T-cell lymphoma than IBD.

## Source-Supported Conclusions

- Ultrasound belongs in the boundary workup branch as a real contributor, not as decorative background.
- Muscularis thickening should be modeled as a lymphoma-leaning signal rather than a generic inflammatory-intestinal abnormality.
- Lymphadenopathy is less discriminative because it raises abnormal-disease suspicion for both lymphoma and IBD.
- Imaging can pressure the workup toward stronger lymphoma concern, but it should not replace tissue sampling.

## What It Does Not Prove

- It does not prove muscularis thickening alone can diagnose lymphoma.
- It does not resolve the inflammatory-versus-neoplastic boundary without biopsy and histopathology.
- It does not make every older cat with muscularis thickening a lymphoma case.
