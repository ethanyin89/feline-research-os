# src-ibd-003 Deep Extraction Round 1

## Core Takeaway

`feline IBD should be treated as a diagnosis-of-exclusion architecture, not as a default endpoint label`

## What This Source Most Strongly Adds

- a broad top-level framing anchor for the whole IBD module
- a warning against overdiagnosing IBD before excluding food-responsive and neoplastic mimics
- early support for careful biopsy acquisition and expert pathology interpretation
- bounded treatment relevance through diet, but not a treatment-first worldview

## Quoted / Near-Quoted Facts

- The abstract describes feline IBD as a diagnosis of exclusion.
- The abstract states that dietary intolerance or allergy can have the same clinical and histologic appearance as IBD.
- The abstract states that well-differentiated alimentary lymphosarcoma can also be confused with IBD.
- The abstract states that intestinal biopsies are useful but must be taken carefully and interpreted by someone with expertise in alimentary tract pathology.
- The abstract states that well-constructed dietary therapy can often benefit both dietary problems and IBD.

## Source-Supported Conclusions

- The broad IBD module should be organized around exclusion, not around one default syndrome label.
- The `IBD versus small-cell / alimentary lymphoma` boundary is not a late refinement problem; it is present in the review-level framing from the start.
- Biopsy belongs in the core workup branch, but biopsy quality and pathology interpretation should be modeled as limitations as well as strengths.
- Diet belongs in the practical branch early, but the review does not justify flattening treatment into one simple drug-first or immune-suppression-first story.

## What It Does Not Prove

- It does not prove one clean diagnostic biomarker for feline IBD.
- It does not solve the full distinction between dietary disease, idiopathic inflammation, and lymphoma.
- It does not provide modern fibrosis, metabolomic, or stool-biomarker authority.
