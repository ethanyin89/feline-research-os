# src-ibd-001 Deep Extraction Round 1

## Core Takeaway

`microbiota differences can pressure the IBD-versus-lymphoma boundary without becoming a first-line diagnostic test`

## What This Source Most Strongly Adds

- the first microbiota branch that directly touches the main disease-boundary problem
- support for treating dysbiosis as disease-structure information rather than generic background
- a bridge between bacterial shifts and mucosal inflammatory signaling

## Quoted / Near-Quoted Facts

- The abstract states that the GI microbiota in healthy cats is altered in IBD.
- The study included 14 cats with IBD and 14 cats with small-cell GI lymphoma.
- The abstract states that Enterobacteriaceae were more abundant in small-cell GI lymphoma than in IBD.
- The abstract states that Bifidobacterium spp., Bacteroides spp., and Prevotella spp. were less abundant in small-cell GI lymphoma than in IBD.
- The methods correlated bacterial groups identified by FISH in GI biopsies with CD11b-positive and NF-kappaB expression.

## Source-Supported Conclusions

- The microbiota branch should not be modeled only as generic IBD dysbiosis; it also touches the lymphoma boundary.
- Microbial shifts may help explain biologic separation between inflammatory and neoplastic intestinal disease even if they are not yet routine clinical tools.
- Mechanism and boundary interpretation can be linked through mucosal bacterial and inflammatory-signaling data.
- The module should keep `microbiota depth` below `biopsy and histopathology authority` in the practical workup hierarchy.

## What It Does Not Prove

- It does not prove a microbiota test can routinely distinguish IBD from lymphoma in practice.
- It does not prove these bacterial shifts cause lymphoma.
- It does not replace biopsy, imaging, or pathology interpretation.
