## Phase 0: Sequential Micro-Analysis

#### Unit 1: Why older-cat surveillance matters before CKD becomes obvious

- core_claim: the guideline treats CKD detection as an ongoing surveillance problem in older cats, not as a one-time diagnostic event after severe illness appears.
- implicit_premise: earlier recognition is clinically useful enough to justify repeated health checks and selected diagnostic testing in cats older than 7 years.
- relation_to_previous: opening practical frame for the guideline's diagnostic logic.
- hard_details: health checks every 6 months for cats `>7 years`; annual selected diagnostic testing; evaluation includes body weight, body condition score, blood pressure, haematology, serum biochemistry, and routine urinalysis.
- tension_or_surprise: the guideline assumes operational follow-up discipline that may not exist consistently in real practice.

#### Unit 2: How routine CKD diagnosis is actually made in practice

- core_claim: feline CKD is usually diagnosed through a combination of azotaemia, inappropriately low USG, and evidence that the abnormalities are sustained.
- implicit_premise: no single simple biomarker currently solves the renal-function problem well enough to replace combined interpretation.
- relation_to_previous: deepens the surveillance frame into practical diagnostic criteria.
- hard_details: creatinine `>140 µmol/l (>1.6 mg/dl)`; USG `<1.035`; sustained change over weeks to months; persistent creatinine increase `>15%` from baseline can indicate reduced renal function.
- tension_or_surprise: the guideline simultaneously relies on creatinine and warns that creatinine is insensitive early and shaped by muscle mass and hydration.

#### Unit 3: Why some CKD cases still escape the classic criteria

- core_claim: the standard azotaemia plus low-USG approach misses some important CKD presentations, especially non-azotaemic or structurally evident disease.
- implicit_premise: structural change, persistent proteinuria, and serial change over time may reveal disease earlier than classic diagnostic thresholds.
- relation_to_previous: qualifies and complicates the routine diagnostic rule.
- hard_details: chronic kidney damage may be present on imaging or via persistent renal-origin proteinuria without azotaemia or low USG; some cats lose concentrating ability before overt azotaemia.
- tension_or_surprise: the guideline is more conservative than a simple threshold-based algorithm, because it keeps diagnosis open even when the classic pair of markers is incomplete.

#### Unit 4: What the minimum routine database is trying to accomplish

- core_claim: the routine workup is not just for confirming CKD, but for identifying cause, complications, and concomitant disease that will change management.
- implicit_premise: CKD management quality depends on context, not on renal markers alone.
- relation_to_previous: moves from diagnosis to structured staging and workup.
- hard_details: minimum routine database includes full history and physical examination, urinalysis with `USG`, dipstick, sediment, `UPCR`, culture where indicated, serum biochemistry including electrolytes and phosphate, haematology, `SBP`, diagnostic imaging, and sometimes biopsy/FNA; aims include identifying aetiology, complications, and concomitant disease such as hyperthyroidism.
- tension_or_surprise: this section makes clear that “renal workup” in the guideline is broader than many minimal clinical checklists.

#### Unit 5: Advanced and emerging tests still do not replace the core workflow

- core_claim: GFR and SDMA matter, but the guideline does not treat either as a standalone replacement for the practical diagnostic core.
- implicit_premise: early-detection tools must still prove clinical accuracy and usefulness before displacing serial routine markers.
- relation_to_previous: extends the routine workup into advanced testing.
- hard_details: GFR remains the gold standard but is used mainly to confirm suspected CKD in non-azotaemic cats; SDMA appears more sensitive than creatinine for early detection and is not affected by muscle mass; further studies are required; SDMA cannot currently be recommended as a single screening test; may support diagnosis or staging, especially with marked muscle loss.
- tension_or_surprise: the guideline gives SDMA real promise while explicitly refusing to let it become a one-test shortcut.

#### Unit 6: Management priorities are evidence-graded and diet-centered

- core_claim: the guideline frames management as evidence-graded care in which renal diets and phosphorus control sit unusually high in the hierarchy.
- implicit_premise: supportive care is not flat; some interventions have materially stronger evidence than others.
- relation_to_previous: shifts from diagnosis/staging into treatment prioritisation.
- hard_details: renal diets reduce uraemic signs and prolong longevity; reported median survival from cited studies includes `264 vs 633` days, `210 vs 480` days, and `~730 vs not reached`; phosphate restriction is thought to drive much of the longevity benefit; phosphate binders are important when diet alone is insufficient; examples listed include aluminium hydroxide/carbonate `90 mg/kg`, calcium carbonate `90 mg/kg`, calcium acetate `60–90 mg/kg`, sevelamer `90–160 mg/kg`, lanthanum `30–90 mg/kg`; binders should be split and given with food.
- tension_or_surprise: the guideline gives unusually concrete operational dosing detail at the same time that many higher-level summaries of CKD treatment stay vague.

## Phase 1: Theme Reconstruction

## Theme: Surveillance before obvious disease

The guideline treats CKD in older cats as a condition that should be actively looked for rather than passively discovered. It recommends health checks every 6 months in cats older than 7 years and at least annual selected diagnostic testing. This means the authors assume that useful diagnostic signal exists before the disease becomes clinically dramatic. The logic is practical rather than theoretical: watch older cats repeatedly, track change over time, and do not wait for severe illness before asking whether CKD is present.

### Key Quote
> health checks every 6 months for cats >7 years of age

### Hard Information

- `>7 years`
- 6-month health checks
- annual selected diagnostic testing
- weight, body condition, blood pressure, haematology, biochemistry, urinalysis

## Theme: Practical diagnosis is a composite judgment, not a single marker

The guideline does not present a magic test. Instead, it says feline CKD is commonly diagnosed through the combination of increased creatinine, inappropriately low USG, and evidence that this picture is sustained over time. It also explicitly warns that this framework is imperfect: creatinine changes can be small early in disease, muscle mass and hydration matter, and some cats show structural or urinary abnormalities before obvious azotaemia. That makes serial measurement and contextual interpretation central to the whole diagnostic method.

### Key Quote
> a simple, accurate biomarker to assess renal function does not currently exist

### Hard Information

- creatinine `>140 µmol/l`
- USG `<1.035`
- sustained change over weeks to months
- `>15%` persistent creatinine increase from baseline

## Theme: The workup is broad because management depends on context

The minimum routine database is not only a CKD confirmation tool. It is designed to identify possible underlying causes, complications arising from CKD, and concomitant disease that may alter management. That is why UPCR, SBP, imaging, culture where indicated, and broader biochemistry matter. The guideline is effectively saying that a renal case is also a comorbidity-management case.

### Key Quote
> identifying potential underlying aetiologies ... complications ... concomitant disease

### Hard Information

- UPCR
- SBP
- imaging
- hyperthyroidism named as management-relevant concomitant disease

## Theme: Advanced tests help, but do not replace disciplined core practice

The guideline values GFR and SDMA, but it does so cautiously. GFR remains the gold standard and is mainly used to confirm suspected CKD in non-azotaemic cats. SDMA is presented as promising and less affected by muscle mass, but not yet trustworthy enough to serve as a single screening test. The result is a layered diagnostic logic: serial routine markers first, advanced tests as support.

### Key Quote
> it cannot currently be recommended as a single screening test for CKD

### Hard Information

- GFR gold standard
- SDMA more sensitive than creatinine for early detection
- SDMA not affected by muscle mass
- SDMA may support diagnosis/staging, especially with muscle loss

## Theme: Management is evidence-graded and phosphorus control matters more than it first appears

The guideline gives renal diet an unusually privileged place because it links diet to reduced uraemic signs and longer survival. It also argues that phosphate restriction may be a major reason the benefit exists, and that phosphate binders become important when diet alone is insufficient. This matters for the vault because it reinforces phosphorus as a bridge variable spanning mechanism, endpoint interpretation, and treatment.

### Key Quote
> renal diets have been shown to ... significantly prolong longevity

### Hard Information

- survival comparisons: `264 vs 633`, `210 vs 480`, `~730 vs not reached`
- phosphate binders: aluminium hydroxide/carbonate `90 mg/kg`, calcium carbonate `90 mg/kg`, calcium acetate `60–90 mg/kg`, sevelamer `90–160 mg/kg`, lanthanum `30–90 mg/kg`
- binders split and given with food
