## Phase 0: Sequential Micro-Analysis

#### Unit 1: This is a signalment-risk paper, not a clinicopathology paper

- core_claim: the study asks whether age, breed, sex, and neuter status are associated with naturally occurring FIP in Australia.
- implicit_premise: recognition begins before clinicopathology, at the level of who should trigger earlier suspicion.
- relation_to_previous: opening branch placement.
- hard_details: 382 confirmed FIP cases were compared with the Companion Animal Register of NSW and the general cat population of Sydney.
- tension_or_surprise: this paper strengthens recognition by sharpening pretest suspicion, not by improving diagnostic certainty.

#### Unit 2: Age is still the strongest practical risk signal

- core_claim: younger cats are significantly over-represented among FIP cases.
- implicit_premise: age should remain one of the leading operational risk-context variables in the module.
- relation_to_previous: moves from design to dominant finding.
- hard_details: the abstract explicitly identifies younger cats as significantly over-represented.
- tension_or_surprise: age remains one of the simplest but most persistent recognition levers.

#### Unit 3: Breed signal is structured, not just “pedigree cats”

- core_claim: the paper shows both over-represented and under-represented breed groups rather than one blunt pedigree effect.
- implicit_premise: breed risk should be modeled as patterned susceptibility, not as a generic purebred warning.
- relation_to_previous: refines risk granularity.
- hard_details: domestic crossbred, Persian, and Himalayan cats were under-represented; British Shorthair, Devon Rex, and Abyssinian were over-represented.
- tension_or_surprise: the branch becomes more specific than the common phrase `pedigree predisposition`.

#### Unit 4: Male over-representation matters, but should stay below age and ecology

- core_claim: male cats were significantly over-represented among FIP cases.
- implicit_premise: sex belongs in risk context, but should not overshadow stronger age and environment signals.
- relation_to_previous: adds another signalment layer.
- hard_details: the abstract reports a significantly higher proportion of male cats with FIP than female cats.
- tension_or_surprise: this is useful for recognition weighting, but not decisive by itself.

#### Unit 5: The study pushes genetics into the recognition branch without turning recognition into genetics

- core_claim: the paper points toward host genetic factors while remaining a practical recognition source first.
- implicit_premise: signalment patterns can pressure mechanism hypotheses without replacing clinical recognition architecture.
- relation_to_previous: connects epidemiology to broader module logic.
- hard_details: the abstract states that the findings open further avenues to investigate the role of genetic factors in FIP.
- tension_or_surprise: recognition data here also pressures mechanism thinking, but should still be used mainly as pretest context.

## Phase 1: Theme Reconstruction

## Theme: Signalment risk meaningfully shapes pretest suspicion

This paper matters because it gives the risk branch a clearer answer to `who should make you worry earlier` before any one test is considered.

### Hard Information

- 382 confirmed FIP cases
- comparison with NSW register and Sydney general cat population

## Theme: Age and breed are not interchangeable risk signals

Age is broad and operational; breed is patterned and more specific. The module should not compress both into one vague `predisposition` bucket.

### Hard Information

- younger cats over-represented
- British Shorthair, Devon Rex, Abyssinian over-represented
- domestic crossbred, Persian, Himalayan under-represented

## Theme: Risk context sharpens suspicion but does not diagnose

The value of this paper is earlier suspicion, not diagnostic settlement.

### Hard Information

- male over-representation
- signalment associations only
