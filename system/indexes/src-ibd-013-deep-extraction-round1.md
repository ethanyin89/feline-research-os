# src-ibd-013 Deep Extraction Round 1

## Core Takeaway

`vitamin D looks like a shared burden marker, not a boundary discriminator`

## What This Source Most Strongly Adds

- the first serum support-marker anchor spanning IBD and small-cell lymphoma
- a reminder that systemic nutritional or inflammatory burden markers can be abnormal without classifying disease
- a bridge from GI disease burden to wider physiologic consequences

## Quoted / Near-Quoted Facts

- The abstract reports 84 cats total, including healthy controls, hospitalized ill controls, and cats with IBD or intestinal small-cell lymphoma.
- Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D concentrations were significantly lower in cats with IBD or small-cell lymphoma than in healthy cats and hospitalized ill cats.
- There was overlap in vitamin D concentrations among groups.
- In the GI disease group, there was a moderate positive correlation between serum albumin and vitamin D concentrations.

## Source-Supported Conclusions

- Vitamin D belongs in a shared support-marker layer, not in a discriminatory boundary layer.
- The most plausible current role is burden, malabsorption, complication, or prognostic context rather than disease-class separation.
- Serum support markers should sit below biopsy-centered and imaging-centered workup architecture.

## What It Does Not Prove

- It does not prove vitamin D can distinguish IBD from lymphoma.
- It does not prove causality between hypovitaminosis D and disease severity.
- It does not define the best treatment strategy for low vitamin D.
