## Core Takeaway

## What This Source Most Strongly Adds

- the first dedicated idiopathic-IBD-side immunopathology anchor
- support for an inflammatory-imbalance interpretation on the IBD side of the module
- a reminder that idiopathic IBD remains an elimination diagnosis even when tissue cytokine patterns are measured

## Quoted / Near-Quoted Facts

- The abstract states that feline IBD cannot be diagnosed only through histologic findings and remains an elimination diagnosis.
- The study included nine cats with chronic GI signs and healthy control samples.
- TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-12, and CD3-positive expression were significantly different between cats with IBD and controls.
- No differences were found for TGF-beta, IL-10, and FOXP3.
- The abstract concludes that imbalance in immune response may play a role in feline IBD etiopathogenesis.

## Source-Supported Conclusions

- Idiopathic IBD can now be given more specific immunopathology depth on its own side of the module.
- This branch helps explain inflammatory biology without collapsing into diagnostic authority.
- The idiopathic-IBD branch should be kept distinct from the lymphoma-boundary branch even when both use tissue markers.

## What It Does Not Prove

- It does not prove a practical immunohistochemical diagnostic panel for routine IBD diagnosis.
- It does not separate idiopathic IBD from lymphoma in operational practice.
- It does not replace broader exclusion-based workup.
