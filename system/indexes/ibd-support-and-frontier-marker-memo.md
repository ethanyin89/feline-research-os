# IBD Support And Frontier Marker Memo

## Core Takeaway

`support markers and frontier markers should not be collapsed into one biomarker tier`

## Current Marker Hierarchy

- `vitamin D` belongs to a shared systemic burden or complication-context layer
- `fecal S100A12` belongs to a noninvasive disease-versus-health inflammatory support layer
- `metabolomics` belongs to the strongest current frontier-separation layer
- `fibrosis` should sit outside the biomarker ladder as a structural chronicity layer; it tracks burden and histopathologic assessment, not routine class separation

## What The Module Can Already Say

- low vitamin D and elevated fecal S100A12 both indicate abnormal disease burden more than disease class
- metabolomics is the strongest current candidate for future class separation among these three branches
- even the strongest frontier marker still sits below exclusion-first workup, imaging, biopsy strategy, and pathology
- fibrosis is common enough in chronic inflammatory enteropathy to be modeled explicitly, and `src-ibd-022` ties it to lower body weight and serum albumin rather than leaving it as descriptive histology
- fibrosis should be used to deepen chronicity and burden architecture, not to flatten support markers, frontier markers, and structural remodeling into one evidence tier

## Current Boundaries

- `shared abnormality` is not the same thing as `class discriminator`
- `noninvasive support` is not the same thing as `frontier separation`
- `frontier separation` is not the same thing as `routine readiness`
- `structural chronicity` is not the same thing as `antifibrotic treatment readiness`
