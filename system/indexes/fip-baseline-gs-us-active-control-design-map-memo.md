# Baseline GS-441524 U.S. Active-Control Design Map Memo

Date: `2026-04-21`

Scope: first-pass study-design map for the baseline GS-441524 U.S. regulatory branch.

This memo is not a protocol, FDA advice, or a claim that FDA would accept any specific design.

It does one narrower thing:

`map what an active-control effectiveness design would have to solve before the U.S. route branch becomes decision-grade`

## Starting Point

The [baseline GS U.S. conditional-approval eligibility memo](fip-baseline-gs-us-conditional-approval-eligibility-memo.md) holds expanded conditional approval as:

`plausible-to-test, not established`

That result created the next question:

`if untreated or placebo-controlled FIP studies are ethically or practically difficult, what does active-control planning need to handle?`

FDA GFI #204 is relevant because it discusses active controls in companion-animal effectiveness studies.

It does not prove that active-control design is acceptable for this product.

## Product Object

Design work should stay anchored to the same first-pass object:

`baseline GS-441524 antiviral product for non-neurologic naturally occurring FIP`

The design map should not start with:

- severe neurologic / ocular FIP
- remdesivir plus GS-441524 package logic
- all-form cure claims
- compounded-access policy
- GC376

## Core Takeaway

`Active-control design is the right next U.S. planning object, but the current vault cannot yet choose a pivotal design.`

The reason is specific:

- a placebo or untreated concurrent control may be ethically and practically fragile after modern GS-era treatment evidence
- an active-control design needs a credible comparator, effect margin, endpoint definition, and rescue rule
- the current vault has treatment-transformation evidence, but not a sponsor-grade comparator package
- the [U.S. comparator boundary memo](fip-baseline-gs-us-comparator-boundary-memo.md) now shows why compounded GS-441524 is access reality rather than a clean active comparator

## Design Forks

| Design Fork | Why It Is Tempting | Main Problem | Current Safe Read |
|---|---|---|---|
| Placebo / untreated concurrent control | cleanest traditional contrast against no antiviral effect | may be ethically hard in a rapidly fatal disease when antiviral evidence exists | not impossible from this vault alone, but should not be the default planning assumption |
| Active-control superiority | can avoid untreated control while still asking whether product improves on an active comparator | requires a legitimate active comparator and a superiority hypothesis | possible in principle, but no clean comparator is established in the vault |
| Active-control non-inferiority | may fit a product seeking to show comparable effectiveness with better product quality / access / label discipline | requires a justified non-inferiority margin and reliable historical active-control effect | currently too thin for a confident design recommendation |
| Add-on / rescue design | can preserve animal welfare through rescue criteria | rescue may confound primary effectiveness unless prespecified carefully | useful as a safety / ethics component, not a complete design answer |
| Single-arm with external / historical control | attractive where disease is rare or fatal and treatment effect is large | regulatory strength depends on comparability and bias control | supportive for conditional logic, weak as a full-approval plan unless FDA precedent supports it |

## Comparator Problem

The comparator is the hardest design issue.

A clean active-control plan needs an active control whose effect is sufficiently established and whose product identity is stable.

Current vault state:

- `src-fip-016` supports baseline GS-441524 efficacy and safety in naturally occurring FIP.
- `src-fip-019` supports real-world remdesivir plus GS package logic, but it is not the same product object.
- `src-fip-024` supports neurologic rescue complexity, but it is outside the first baseline label.
- `src-reg-014` states that FDA had no approved FIP treatment drug as of the May 10, 2024 GS-441524 update and places compounded GS-441524 in an unapproved / enforcement-discretion frame.
- Compounded or informal-market GS products are not clean regulatory comparators inside the current source set.

Safe comparator read:

`the active-control question is real, but comparator identity is not yet solved`

## Endpoint Problem

The current FIP endpoint page already separates diagnosis support from treatment follow-up.

For U.S. effectiveness design, the primary endpoint family should likely come from treatment follow-up, not initial diagnosis.

Candidate endpoint components:

- survival through the planned treatment period
- clinical remission / response after a defined course
- completion of the planned treatment course without failure
- relapse or retreatment status after treatment
- rescue-treatment requirement
- adverse events and discontinuation

The strongest current paper-level anchor is `src-fip-016`, but the vault still lacks full endpoint definitions, censoring rules, relapse thresholds, and statistical analysis logic.

Unsafe endpoint language:

`24 healthy cats at publication is already a pivotal endpoint definition`

Safe endpoint language:

`src-fip-016 identifies plausible endpoint components, but not a sponsor-grade endpoint architecture`

## Inclusion / Exclusion Boundary

The first-pass U.S. active-control map should stay narrower than the whole FIP universe.

Suggested internal boundary:

- include naturally occurring FIP cases matching the baseline GS evidence layer
- stratify effusive / dry-to-effusive / non-effusive disease if included together
- exclude or separately branch severe neurologic and ocular FIP
- require a defined diagnostic composite before enrollment
- prespecify baseline severity and early-death handling

Why this matters:

`the study population is part of the label`

If neurologic rescue or ocular disease is mixed into the first-pass design, dose, monitoring, relapse, and endpoint interpretation change.

## Rescue And Failure Rules

FIP designs need explicit rescue logic because early deterioration, relapse, and retreatment are part of the current treatment evidence.

Rules that need prespecification:

- when deterioration triggers rescue
- whether rescue counts as primary endpoint failure
- whether dose escalation is allowed
- how relapse after apparent response is counted
- whether retreatment success is a secondary success or evidence of first-course failure
- how early death / euthanasia is handled

Current safe read:

`rescue logic is ethically important and endpoint-dangerous at the same time`

## Conditional-To-Full Approval Bridge

Active-control planning matters even if the first U.S. route remains expanded conditional approval.

Reason:

- conditional approval can rest on reasonable expectation of effectiveness
- continued renewal / later full approval still needs progress toward substantial evidence
- a route plan without a later effectiveness design is not durable

This memo therefore treats active-control design as:

`the bridge between plausible conditional eligibility and a credible full-approval evidence path`

## What We Can Say Now

- Active-control planning is a real next step for the U.S. baseline GS branch.
- The current vault supports avoiding simplistic placebo-first assumptions.
- The first design object should stay narrower than all-form FIP.
- Endpoint planning should treat response, remission, relapse, rescue, survival, and safety as separate pieces.
- Comparator identity is the main unsolved design blocker.

## What We Cannot Say Now

- do not say FDA would require or accept an active-control design
- do not choose non-inferiority or superiority yet
- do not use compounded or informal-market products as clean comparators without a separate source pack
- do not treat `src-fip-016` outcome language as a ready pivotal endpoint
- do not mix neurologic rescue into the first baseline design unless the label is redefined
- do not treat active-control planning as proof of conditional-approval eligibility

## Working Design Map

| Design Element | Provisional Internal Choice | Why | Main Open Work |
|---|---|---|---|
| Product object | baseline GS-441524 | cleanest first regulatory stress-test object | product identity / CMC package |
| Population | non-neurologic naturally occurring FIP | matches strongest baseline treatment anchor | diagnostic composite and severity strata |
| Comparator | unresolved active comparator | placebo / untreated may be fragile; active comparator not clean yet | FDA precedent and approved-alternative check |
| Primary endpoint family | treatment success / survival / remission composite | closer to FIP treatment value than diagnostic markers | exact definition, timing, censoring, rescue rules |
| Rescue rules | required but failure-sensitive | animal welfare and endpoint validity both depend on them | prespecified rescue and dose-escalation handling |
| Full-approval bridge | needed from the start | conditional route still needs later substantial evidence | pivotal-design strategy |

## Best Next Work

1. Use the [U.S. comparator boundary memo](fip-baseline-gs-us-comparator-boundary-memo.md) to drive official-record and product-identity checks.
2. Check FDA FIP-specific precedent, advisory records, and Animal Drugs @ FDA / Green Book status.
3. Extract full endpoint definitions from `src-fip-016` if available, especially response, relapse, retreatment, and follow-up timing.
4. Create a narrow label-population memo for baseline GS-441524.
5. Create a data-package gap memo for CMC, target-animal safety, adverse-event reporting, and post-approval monitoring.
