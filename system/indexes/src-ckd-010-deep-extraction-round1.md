# Deep Extraction Worksheet

Source: `src-ckd-010`  
Title: `Histomorphometry of Feline Chronic Kidney Disease and Correlation With Markers of Renal Dysfunction`  
Method note: this worksheet is based on the abstract and visible methods/results text available from the article page.

## Phase 0: Sequential Micro-Analysis

#### Unit 1: Why this study exists

- core_claim: feline CKD lesions are well described, but few studies had quantified them and related them directly to clinicopathological markers of dysfunction.
- implicit_premise: quantifying lesions and correlating them with clinical markers can improve understanding of pathogenesis and progression.
- relation_to_previous: opening rationale for the study.
- hard_details: CKD common in geriatric cats; reported prevalence up to `49%` in cats older than 15 years; prior studies found many nonspecific lesions and only partial identification of specific renal diseases.
- tension_or_surprise: the study is trying to bridge a gap between descriptive pathology and operational clinical interpretation.

#### Unit 2: How the cohort was defined

- core_claim: the authors built the study from cats seen in first-opinion practice and only included cases with postmortem tissue plus recent biochemical data.
- implicit_premise: pairing near-death biochemical data with necropsy pathology can create a clinically meaningful lesion-marker bridge.
- relation_to_previous: moves from rationale into cohort construction.
- hard_details: recruitment through 2 London first-opinion practices from `1992 to 2010`; CKD diagnosis required plasma creatinine `>2.0 mg/dl` plus history/clinical signs compatible with chronic disease; only cats with postmortem data and biochemical profile within last `2 months` of life were included; CKD staged with modified IRIS categories.
- tension_or_surprise: this is not a screening cohort or a prospective treatment trial; it is a necropsy-linked, late-disease correlation design.

#### Unit 3: How proteinuria and blood pressure were operationalized

- core_claim: the study took care to make proteinuria and hypertension data clinically interpretable rather than relying on crude one-time measurements.
- implicit_premise: single-point values can mislead, especially for blood pressure in cats.
- relation_to_previous: deepens the clinical measurement logic.
- hard_details: UPC excluded when bacteriuria or gross hematuria present; Doppler blood pressure used with mean of `5` readings each visit; hypertension diagnosed if `>170 mmHg` on 2 consecutive visits or 1 visit with hypertensive chorioretinopathy; all hypertensive cats started on amlodipine `0.625 mg/day`, increased to `1.25 mg` or exceptionally `2.5 mg`; authors used `time-averaged systolic blood pressure (SBPOT)` instead of last available reading because one value is more vulnerable to white-coat effect or hypovolemia.
- tension_or_surprise: the study's blood-pressure variable is more sophisticated than a simple clinic BP snapshot, which makes the pathology correlation stronger.

#### Unit 4: How lesions were quantified

- core_claim: the study quantified multiple lesion classes rather than giving a purely descriptive pathology label.
- implicit_premise: lesion severity needs to be measured on a structured scale to compare meaningfully with renal dysfunction markers.
- relation_to_previous: shifts from clinical measurements into pathology methods.
- hard_details: sections from randomly selected kidney fixed in formalin; pathologist masked to clinicopathological data; lesions assessed included interstitial fibrosis, interstitial inflammation, glomerulosclerosis, glomerular volume, obsolescent glomeruli, hyperplastic arteriolosclerosis, hyaline arteriolosclerosis, fibrinoid vascular necrosis, and tubular mineralization; fibrosis/inflammation scored `0 to 3` with `0.5` intervals.
- tension_or_surprise: the study is broader than a fibrosis-only paper, even though fibrosis emerges as the dominant lesion.

#### Unit 5: What the correlations actually show

- core_claim: interstitial fibrosis had the strongest relationship with azotemia, hyperphosphatemia, and anaemia, while proteinuria and blood pressure aligned with different structural lesion patterns.
- implicit_premise: different clinical markers are tracing different pieces of renal structural injury rather than one single undifferentiated “kidney severity” axis.
- relation_to_previous: main results section tying the quantified lesions back to clinical markers.
- hard_details: kidneys from `80` cats included; multivariable linear regression used; interstitial fibrosis best correlated with azotemia, hyperphosphatemia, and anaemia; proteinuria associated with interstitial fibrosis and glomerular hypertrophy; higher time-averaged systolic blood pressure associated with glomerulosclerosis and hyperplastic arteriolosclerosis.
- tension_or_surprise: this is strong support for not flattening phosphorus, proteinuria, and SBP into generic monitoring numbers.

## Phase 1: Theme Reconstruction

## Theme: This is a clinicopathology bridge study, not a generic pathology description

The paper is trying to solve a specific problem: feline CKD pathology had been described before, but not systematically quantified and tied back to clinical markers that veterinarians actually use. To do that, the authors built a necropsy-linked cohort from two first-opinion London practices, requiring both postmortem kidney tissue and biochemical data from the last 2 months of life. That design makes the paper especially valuable for the vault because it bridges naturally occurring disease with measurable dysfunction rather than presenting an artificial model.

### Key Quote
> few studies have correlated these lesions with clinicopathological markers of renal dysfunction

### Hard Information

- `1992–2010`
- 2 London first-opinion practices
- creatinine `>2.0 mg/dl`
- biochemical data within `2 months` of life
- modified IRIS staging

## Theme: Blood pressure and proteinuria were handled carefully, not casually

The study does not treat hypertension and proteinuria as simplistic one-time numbers. UPC measurements were filtered to avoid bacteriuria or gross hematuria confounding. Blood pressure used the mean of five Doppler readings per visit, and the analysis relied on time-averaged systolic blood pressure rather than a single late reading. Hypertensive cats were treated with amlodipine, typically starting at 0.625 mg/day and escalating if needed. This is important because it means the SBP-pathology link is built on a more defensible blood-pressure construct.

### Key Quote
> a single blood pressure value is more likely to be affected by “white-coat” hypertension or hypovolemia

### Hard Information

- mean of `5` readings
- hypertension `>170 mmHg`
- amlodipine `0.625`, `1.25`, exceptionally `2.5 mg/day`
- `SBPOT` used for analysis

## Theme: Different markers are tracing different lesion patterns

The core result is not just that “bad kidneys look bad.” It is that specific clinical markers align with specific structural abnormalities. Interstitial fibrosis best tracks azotemia, hyperphosphatemia, and anaemia. Proteinuria maps to interstitial fibrosis and glomerular hypertrophy. Higher time-averaged systolic blood pressure maps to glomerulosclerosis and hyperplastic arteriolosclerosis. This makes the study one of the strongest primary anchors in the vault for saying that phosphorus, proteinuria, and blood pressure are mechanistically informative, not merely convenient monitoring choices.

### Key Quote
> Interstitial fibrosis was the lesion best correlated with the severity of azotemia, hyperphosphatemia, and anemia.

### Hard Information

- `80` cats
- multivariable linear regression
- fibrosis -> azotemia / hyperphosphatemia / anaemia
- proteinuria -> interstitial fibrosis / glomerular hypertrophy
- SBPOT -> glomerulosclerosis / hyperplastic arteriolosclerosis

## Theme: This study supports endpoint consequence mapping, not only endpoint importance

The most reusable upgrade from this paper is not just that common CKD markers correlate with pathology.
It is that different markers appear to carry different structural consequences.

That means the vault can push beyond:

- “these markers matter”

toward:

- “these markers matter in different ways because they map to different lesion axes”

### Hard Information

- fibrosis is the dominant bridge for azotemia, hyperphosphatemia, and anaemia
- proteinuria has a stronger glomerular-hypertrophy and fibrosis-facing profile
- time-averaged blood pressure has a more glomerulo-vascular lesion profile

- implicit_premise: structured quantification is necessary to make lesion-marker comparisons useful

### Marker-specific interpretation Key Points

**Claim 1**  
- support: fibrosis aligns best with azotemia, hyperphosphatemia, and anaemia
- details: multivariable regression; 80-cat cohort
- implicit_premise: these markers carry structural-disease information, not just general illness noise

**Claim 2**  
- support: proteinuria and blood pressure map to different lesion patterns
- details: proteinuria -> fibrosis + glomerular hypertrophy; SBPOT -> glomerulosclerosis + hyperplastic arteriolosclerosis
- implicit_premise: endpoint interpretation should remain multi-axis rather than single-marker

**Claim 3**
- support: phosphorus, anaemia, proteinuria, and blood pressure do not appear to sit on one single downstream severity line
- details: fibrosis-linked and glomerulo-vascular-linked correlations separate them
- implicit_premise: outcome architecture should distinguish endpoint consequence, not only endpoint frequency of use

### Measurement quality Key Points

**Claim 1**  
- support: blood pressure was handled using repeated measures and time-averaging
- details: 5 readings per visit; `>170 mmHg`; amlodipine treatment; SBPOT
- implicit_premise: better hemodynamic measurement gives a more believable pathology association
