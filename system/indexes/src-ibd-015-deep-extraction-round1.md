# src-ibd-015 Deep Extraction Round 1

## Core Takeaway

`site selection is part of the diagnosis, not just part of the procedure`

## What This Source Most Strongly Adds

- the strongest current biopsy-site utility anchor in the IBD module
- a practical warning against treating duodenal biopsy alone as interchangeable with paired duodenal plus ileal workup
- concrete evidence that the lymphoma boundary is partly a sampling problem, not only a pathology-interpretation problem

## Quoted / Near-Quoted Facts

- The abstract reports 70 client-owned cats with gastrointestinal disease and adequate duodenal and ileal tissue biopsies obtained endoscopically.
- The study re-evaluated samples under blinded review by a single pathologist according to WSAVA standards.
- The abstract reports that 18 of 70 cats were diagnosed with small-cell lymphoma in duodenum, ileum, or both.
- Of these 18 cats, 7 were diagnosed only in duodenum, 8 only in ileum, and 3 in both.
- The abstract reports poor agreement between duodenal and ileal diagnoses, with kappa = 0.23.
- The abstract concludes that there is a population of cats in which small-cell lymphoma can be diagnosed only by evaluating ileal biopsies.

## Source-Supported Conclusions

- The IBD-versus-small-cell-lymphoma boundary should be modeled partly as a biopsy-site selection problem.
- Ileal biopsy should not be treated as optional background when the goal is meaningful lymphoma exclusion.
- Histopathology utility remains real, but negative or nonlymphoma findings from one site should be bounded carefully.
- The workup architecture should separate `convenience of duodenal sampling` from `diagnostic completeness`.

## What It Does Not Prove

- It does not prove that every cat requires ileal biopsy in every context.
- It does not eliminate pathologist-interpretation variability or retrospective-study limitations.
- It does not by itself settle all inflammatory versus neoplastic distinctions once tissue is obtained.
