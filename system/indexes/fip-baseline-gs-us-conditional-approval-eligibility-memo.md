# Baseline GS-441524 U.S. Conditional-Approval Eligibility Test Memo

Date: `2026-04-21`

Scope: first-pass U.S. eligibility test for the baseline GS-441524 FIP antiviral archetype.

This memo is not FDA advice, a legal route recommendation, or a sponsor submission strategy.

It does one narrower thing:

`test whether expanded conditional approval is plausible enough to keep as a live U.S. route branch`

## Product Object Being Tested

The tested object is:

`baseline GS-441524 antiviral product for non-neurologic naturally occurring FIP`

This object comes from the [FIP antiviral product-archetype route boundary memo](fip-antiviral-product-archetype-route-boundary-memo.md).

It deliberately does not include:

- remdesivir plus GS-441524 package logic
- severe neurologic / ocular rescue claims
- broad all-form FIP cure language
- compounded-drug access logic
- GC376 or other antiviral archetypes

## Core Takeaway

`U.S. expanded conditional approval is plausible-to-test for baseline GS-441524, but not established.`

The positive case is stronger than it was for chronic companion-animal indications because FIP is serious, life-threatening, and historically treatment-poor.

The limiting case is also strong:

- cats are a major species
- conditional approval is not a general shortcut for major-species feline products
- FDA still requires safety, manufacturing, labeling, and reasonable expectation of effectiveness
- a later full-approval effectiveness plan must remain credible
- the current vault has not ingested FDA FIP-specific approval precedent, but it now has FDA's 2024 compounded-GS position source

## U.S. Route Fork

Current U.S. framework source anchors:

- `src-reg-004`: full approval versus conditional approval fork for animal drugs
- `src-reg-005`: cats as a major species; expanded conditional approval criteria
- `src-reg-006`: active-control study-design guidance for companion-animal effectiveness studies

The live route fork is:

1. **Full approval / NADA path**
   - requires substantial evidence of effectiveness
   - likely needs a prospective effectiveness package with a defensible control design
2. **Expanded conditional approval path**
   - only live if the major-species statutory basis can be argued
   - requires reasonable expectation of effectiveness, not full substantial evidence at first approval
   - still requires safety, proper manufacturing, and truthful label discipline

## Positive Eligibility Case

The best internal argument for keeping expanded conditional approval alive is:

1. FIP is a serious and life-threatening feline disease.
2. The current U.S. regulatory framework allows expanded conditional approval for some major-species products when serious / life-threatening conditions or unmet needs combine with complex or particularly difficult effectiveness studies.
3. The baseline GS-441524 evidence is not speculative only: it includes experimental antiviral foundation and natural-disease efficacy / safety evidence.
4. Untreated or placebo-controlled effectiveness studies may be ethically difficult in the modern treatment era, making active-control design logic relevant.

This is enough to justify a focused U.S. eligibility workstream.

It is not enough to say the product is eligible.

## Main Eligibility Constraints

### Constraint 1: Cats Are Major Species

The cats point cuts both ways.

It makes the FIP product commercially and clinically important, but it also blocks any assumption that conditional approval is easy.

The test must be expanded conditional approval or minor-use-in-major-species logic.

It cannot be written as:

`cats are pets, so conditional approval should be available`

### Constraint 2: Reasonable Expectation Is Not The Whole Package

The current treatment evidence can support a reasonable-expectation discussion.

It cannot replace:

- target animal safety package
- product chemistry / manufacturing / quality package
- stability and batch-consistency evidence
- final route of administration and dosage form
- label population and exclusion rules
- adverse-event monitoring plan

### Constraint 3: Baseline GS Is Not Neurologic Rescue

The clean test object is non-neurologic naturally occurring FIP.

Neurologic FIP remains important, but it changes dose, monitoring, relapse interpretation, and likely label complexity.

The U.S. route test should not use neurologic rescue evidence to broaden the first label unless the claim is deliberately redefined.

### Constraint 4: Active-Control Logic Is A Design Tool, Not A Route Guarantee

`src-reg-006` is useful because it shows that FDA has an active-control study-design frame for companion animals.

It does not say:

`active control design means conditional approval is available`

The right reuse is narrower:

`if placebo or untreated designs are not acceptable, active-control design becomes part of the U.S. evidence strategy`

## Decision Boundary

### What We Can Say Now

- U.S. conditional-approval eligibility is a live question for baseline GS-441524.
- FIP's seriousness and unmet-need profile make this test more plausible than for many chronic feline indications.
- The current evidence base supports internal route-fit testing for a narrow baseline product object.
- Active-control design should be developed alongside any conditional-approval argument.

### What We Cannot Say Now

- do not say FDA would grant expanded conditional approval
- do not say the baseline GS evidence is sponsor-grade
- do not say conditional approval is the preferred U.S. route
- do not use severe neurologic / ocular cases to widen the baseline label without a separate branch
- do not treat treatment efficacy as legal marketing status
- do not treat compounded access or real-world use as FDA approval

## Working Judgment

`hold as plausible-to-test`

Expanded conditional approval should remain a live U.S. route branch for the baseline GS-441524 archetype, but only as a testable hypothesis.

The fallback is not failure; it is an active-control full-approval planning branch.
